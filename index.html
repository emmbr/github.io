Chapter 01 · The Big Picture

What the World Sells — and How That Has Changed

A 35-year panorama of global merchandise exports reveals not just what countries make, but how industrialisation, oil cycles, and the rise of China have continuously redrawn the map of comparative advantage.

Global merchandise trade today is dominated by a single category: Machinery and Electronics, which accounts for roughly a quarter of all world exports. Yet this dominance is relatively recent. In 1988, Fuels held a share nearly equal to Machinery, and commodities — food, raw materials, metals — together represented a much larger slice of the global export basket than they do today.

The chart below tells this story in full. Each coloured band is a sector; its height traces the sector's share of world exports in every year from 1988 to 2023. The compression and expansion of bands over time encodes three decades of economic history.

Fig. 1.1 · World Export Composition
How the global export basket has shifted over 35 years

Stacked area chart showing each sector's share of total world merchandise exports. Hover to inspect individual year values. Note the Fuels spike around 2008 and 2012, and the steady rise of Machinery since the mid-1990s.

When Fuels dominate exports, a country's fortunes are hostage to a single commodity price. When Machinery leads, the story is one of accumulated skill, capital, and complexity.

The 2008 oil price spike is unmistakeable: Fuels briefly ballooned to nearly 20% of world exports before collapsing. The 2022 energy shock — driven by the war in Ukraine — produced a second, sharper spike that has since partially unwound. These twin shocks bracket what analysts call the era of "peak commodity": a period when raw materials briefly reasserted their primacy over manufactured goods.

Feature extraction — Fuels volatility The Fuels band is the most volatile in the entire chart. Its standard deviation across years (≈ 3.2 pp) is more than three times that of the next most volatile sector (Metals, ≈ 0.9 pp). Every other sector is remarkably stable by comparison.

Chapter 02 · Who Trades What

The Geography of Comparative Advantage

Behind global aggregates lie stark national specialisations. A country's trade balance in each sector — the gap between what it exports and what it imports — is the clearest signal of where its competitive advantage truly lies.

The interactive map below encodes trade balance as colour: blue countries are net exporters in a given sector, red countries are net importers. Use the sector slider to flip between all 16 product groups. The patterns that emerge are often striking in their clarity.

Fuels produces the most polarised map of any sector — a handful of deep-blue exporters (Gulf states, Russia, Norway, Angola) surrounded by a sea of red. Machinery inverts the picture almost exactly: Germany, China, Japan, and South Korea dominate the blue; the resource-rich world turns red.

How to read this map Colour = Export Share (%) minus Import Share (%) in percentage points. A value of +20 means a country's exports in this sector are 20 percentage points higher than its imports as a share of total trade. Zero (white) = balanced.
Fig. 2.1 · Interactive Trade Balance Map
Net exporter (blue) vs net importer (red) by sector — use the slider

Trade balance = Export Product Share (%) − Import Product Share (%). Data for 163 countries, 2022. Slide through sectors to reveal each country's specialisation pattern. Grey = no data available.

No country exports everything and imports nothing. Comparative advantage is not a policy choice — it is revealed by trade flows themselves.
Chapter 03 · Winners & Losers

Which Sectors Are Growing, Which Are Fading

Comparative advantage is not fixed. Over three decades, countries have gained and lost export share in sectors — driven by industrialisation, deindustrialisation, demographic shifts, and deliberate industrial policy.

The heatmap below compresses an entire career of structural change into a single glance. Green cells indicate sectors where a country's export share has been rising over the full time period; brown indicates decline. The intensity encodes the speed of change.

China's row tells perhaps the most dramatic story: a steep rise in Machinery and Electronics, and a simultaneous retreat from Textiles and Clothing — a textbook industrial upgrade, played out over three decades of data. India shows a different pattern: services-led growth not fully captured here, but with modest gains in Chemicals and a persistent strength in Textiles.

Chapter 04 · Denmark in Context

A Small, Open Economy with a Distinctive Export Identity

Denmark punches above its weight in global trade. With a population of just 6 million, it maintains a distinctive export identity centred on food, pharmaceuticals, and specialised industrial goods — a profile that sets it apart from its larger European neighbours.

The radar chart below overlays Denmark's export mix against the world average and selected peers. Use the dropdown to swap the comparison country. The further a sector's vertex extends beyond the world average ring, the more Denmark over-indexes in that category relative to global norms.

Animal products and Food are the most striking overweights: Denmark exports nearly three times the world-average share in animal products, reflecting a pork and dairy industry disproportionate to the country's size. Chemicals — driven by Novo Nordisk and the pharma cluster — have been growing as a share for two decades.

Fig. 4.1 · Export Mix Radar
Denmark vs selected peers — use the dropdown to compare

Each axis represents one sector. Values are export share (%). The dashed line shows the world average; the solid fill shows the selected country. Select a comparison country from the dropdown.

Denmark's export identity is not an accident of geography. It reflects a century of deliberate agricultural co-operative structures, pharmaceutical investment, and engineering excellence.

Compared to Germany, Denmark is markedly less machinery-intensive and more food-intensive. Compared to Norway, Denmark exports far less in Fuels — its North Sea reserves are substantially smaller — but far more in processed food. Against the United States, the contrast in Transport equipment (aircraft, vehicles) is stark: Denmark has no mass-market auto or aerospace industry, while the US exports heavily in both.

Key Takeaways

  • Machinery & Electronics now accounts for 26% of world exports — up from ~22% in 1988, making it the dominant sector by a wide margin.
  • Fuels is the most volatile sector in global trade, with a standard deviation three times higher than any other sector across the time series.
  • Animal products and Food sectors are the most positively correlated pair (r = +0.85) — agri-food rises and falls as one.
  • Chemicals and Fuels are the most negatively correlated (r = −0.68): energy price spikes compress chemical output's trade share.
  • China's export shift from Textiles to Machinery is the most structurally significant national trend in the dataset.
  • Denmark over-indexes in Animal products and Food by nearly 3× the world average — a persistent signature of agricultural specialisation.
  • 163 countries show sharply different trade balances across sectors — comparative advantage is geographically concentrated, not evenly distributed.
  • Trade balance maps reveal that Fuels and Machinery are near-perfect mirror images of each other: one country's surplus is another's deficit.

Methodological Note All trade share data are drawn from the World Bank WITS API via the world_trade_data Python package. Sector correlations are Pearson r on first-differenced export share series to remove non-stationarity (cf. Balassa, 1965 for RCA interpretation). Trend slopes are OLS coefficients with years standardised (μ=0, σ=1) for cross-sector comparability. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index measures export product concentration per reporter country; higher values indicate dependence on fewer product categories.
